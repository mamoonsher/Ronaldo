RESIDENCIAL HARVESTING

RAIN BAREELS

Rain barrels are typically large containers designed to capture and store rainwater from rooftops via downspouts.
They are often placed directly underneath the downspout or connected to it through a diverter system.
Here are some key points about rain barrels:
Collection: Rain barrels collect rainwater from roofs, preventing runoff that can contribute to soil erosion and water pollution.
Storage: They store rainwater for later use during dry periods, reducing the demand on municipal water supplies or wells.
Benefits:
Environmental: Reduce stormwater runoff and lessen the strain on water resources.
Economic: Save money on water bills by using collected rainwater for outdoor watering needs.
Gardening: Rainwater is typically better for plants than tap water due to its natural pH and lack of chlorine.
Components: A typical rain barrel includes a lid to prevent debris and mosquitoes from entering, an overflow outlet to manage excess water, and a spigot for easy access to the collected water.
Installation: Rain barrels are usually placed on a stable, level surface such as a concrete pad or cinder blocks to elevate them for easier access to the spigot.


DRY SYSTEM


Dry System in Rainwater Harvesting
Definition: A dry system, also known as a "direct" or "non-pressurized" system, refers to a method of rainwater harvesting where rainwater is collected and stored without additional pumps or pressure systems.
Components:
Collection: Rainwater is collected from rooftops and directed via gutters and downspouts into storage containers such as rain barrels or larger cisterns.
Storage: The collected rainwater is stored in these containers without any additional mechanisms for pressurization or distribution.
Characteristics: Gravity-Fed: Water flows from the collection point (roof) to the storage container under the force of gravity. Simplicity: Dry systems are simpler and typically more cost-effective to install and maintain compared to wet systems. Low Maintenance: Since they lack pumps or complex filtration systems, dry systems generally require less maintenance.
Usage:
Outdoor Use: The stored rainwater is primarily used for outdoor purposes such as watering gardens, lawns, or washing cars. Non-Potable Applications: In many cases, rainwater collected in dry systems is used for non-potable applications due to limited filtration and treatment.
Storage Tank: Rainwater is stored in tanks or cisterns typically made of plastic, concrete, or other materials suitable for water storage.
